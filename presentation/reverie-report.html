Full project report · a plain-English companion to the slide deck

Reverie

What we built, how it works inside, and how the project progressed, from the first idea to the finished product. This report follows the same flow as the deck, slide by slide, but slower and in plain words.

Team: Amaly Attia · Em Echeverria · Lea Sarouphim Hochar · Stephan Pentchev · Cecile Tambey  ·  Course: Deep Learning, IE University

In one breath: Reverie reads what you have watched and loved, learns your taste with deep-learning models, and lines up films you will probably like, so you stop scrolling and start watching. It grew over the project from one recurrent model on old movie data into two models, a modern catalogue, a full app, friends, and a "Blend" that picks something two people will both enjoy.

1 · The problem worth solving

Deck: the business case

Streaming gave us everything and made watching harder. A typical household juggles four or more services and tens of thousands of titles, and the average viewer spends roughly ten minutes per session just deciding, then often gives up and watches nothing. For couples and groups, the negotiation is worse.

That wasted time is the user's pain. For the streaming companies, the same thing shows up as churn, people who can't find anything cancel. So a good recommender is not a nicety; it is a retention engine.

What Reverie promises

Import what you have seen, and Reverie ranks the whole catalogue to your taste in under a second. Its high-confidence "you will love it" picks were right every single time on our held-out real viewers (precision 1.00). And the "Blend" finds a film two people will both enjoy, ending the group debate.

Why it matters in money (illustrative): Netflix has publicly credited roughly $1B a year to its recommender through reduced churn. Even a one-point churn cut on a million subscribers at €15/month is about €1.8M a year retained. Reverie is that engine, in miniature.

2 · How the project progressed, start to finish

The project grew in two passes: first the recommender model (a recurrent network on watch sequences), then the product around it (a second model for modern films and a full app). Each step built on the last, nothing was thrown away.

Part A · the recommender model
Phase 1 · idea

Pick a real problem

Choice overload. We decided to build a recommender that predicts your next film.

Phase 2 · design & audit

Plan it, then attack the plan

We wrote a design spec, then ran an "adversarial audit", deliberately hunting for flaws (data leaks, weak evaluation, scope creep) and fixing them before writing code.

Phase 3 · data pipeline

Prepare the data, no cheating

MovieLens (1M ratings). Each history became many "predict the next film" examples, with the test answers carefully hidden so the model can't peek.

Phase 4 · the model (GRU)

Build and tune the recurrent model

We tested how far back to look, how big to make it, and which memory unit, then froze the best, leanest version and ran a final 3-seed test.

Phase 5 · app v1

A working demo

Build a history, get picks, a thumbs-down re-ranks instantly, and a trick to extend recommendations to TV shows it never trained on.

Part B · the product app
Phase 6 · the problem

It only knew old movies

The GRU was trained on films up to the year 2000, so it could never recommend anything modern. Teaching it from one person's ratings just made it memorize.

Phase 7 · a second model

Learn from millions, not one

We trained a new model on 9.9 million Letterboxd ratings to find your "taste neighbours" and recommend modern films.

Phase 8 · the product

Turn it into a real app

Accounts, import your watch history (Letterboxd or Netflix), a watchlist, friends, and a "Blend" for two people, plus a one-line taste summary.

Phase 9 · modern catalogue

Connect new movies + better ranking

~8,000 modern films with posters, served by the new model, and a smarter way to rank (by your favourites, not your average).

Phase 10 · ship

Polish, write up, push

A redesign, five demo profiles, a results notebook, refreshed docs, an animated deck, this report, and a pull request on GitHub.

4 · The two "brains"

Deck: two networks, one product

Reverie ended up with two models, each answering a different question. Quick reminder of the words, then the deep dive.

ELI5ANN vs GRU vs NCF

ANN (artificial neural network) is the basic family, data flows in one direction, no memory. GRU is a network with memory that reads things in order (the RNN family). NCF isn't a new family, it's an ANN used for recommendations, with "embeddings" for users and movies. So our two models are a GRU and an NCF (an ANN).

Model 1 · the GRU

"What film comes next in your sequence?"
  • Reads your history in order
  • Has a memory of earlier films
  • Classification: picks the next film
  • Trained on MovieLens · the original

Model 2 · the NCF

"How much will you like this film?"
  • Learns from everyone's ratings
  • A "taste fingerprint" per person + film
  • Regression: predicts a 1–10 rating
  • Trained on 9.9M ratings · powers the app

5 · Model 1, the GRU (the recurrent network)

Deck: Model 1 · the RNN
ELI5RNN / GRU

A network with a memory that reads a sequence in order. Perfect for a watch history, because what you saw earlier hints at what you want next. A GRU is a small, efficient memory unit with two "gates" that decide what to keep and what to forget.

The recurrent cell

One film goes in at the bottom. The cell mixes it with its memory of everything before, and passes an updated memory up to the next step. That loop, applied film after film, is what "recurrent" means.

The full network

Softmaxnext film

Each film is turned into a small list of numbers (an embedding) plus its genres and your rating. The GRU reads the 20-film window, and a final layer (softmax) gives a probability to every film in the catalogue, the highest one is the prediction. Because it picks an option from a list, this is a classification.

How we trained and tuned it

Deck: Model 1 · unrolled & tuned

Two careful choices made the evaluation honest:

All-prefix training. From one person's history we make many lessons: the first film predicts the second, the first two predict the third, and so on. Tens of thousands of practice questions instead of one.

Leave-one-out testing. We hide each person's very last film and only ever test on that, never letting the model see the answer during training. (Definitions for overfitting, baseline and the scores are in section 9.)

Then we swept the settings on a practice set and locked the winner: look back 20 films, embedding size 32, GRU width 64, GRU rather than the heavier LSTM. We chose these not because they were the absolute highest, but because they scored near the top with the least memorizing and the fewest parts.

6 · Model 2, the NCF (an ANN with embeddings)

Deck: Model 2 · the feed-forward network
ELI5Embedding

Turning a thing, a movie or a person, into a short list of numbers that captures its "vibe". Films co-loved by similar people end up with similar numbers, so the computer can measure how alike two films, or two people's tastes, really are.

ELI5Collaborative filtering & regression

Collaborative filtering: "people with taste like yours also loved this", it learns from millions of ratings, not film descriptions. Regression: instead of picking an option, it guesses a number, here your star rating from 1 to 10.

Each person and each movie gets a 50-number "fingerprint" the model learns from scratch. A small "content tower" adds the film's genres and stats (so a brand-new film still gets a sensible guess). These join and pass through two hidden layers (the "Dense + ReLU" boxes, the part that learns what makes you different), ending in a single predicted rating. The loss it minimizes is MSE, reported as RMSE, "how far off, on average".

A neat design trick: predict the residual

Deck: Model 2 · forward pass & design
rating = global average + user bias + movie bias + network(you, the film)
The first three terms already reproduce the simple averages (this film is generally loved; you rate high). So the neural part only has to learn the leftover, the personal twist, which is the hard, useful bit. This makes training easier and more accurate.

7 · How recommendations are actually served

Deck: serving · the real recommender

Predicting a rating is one thing; turning it into a good ordered list is another. Our first attempt, building a brand-new user vector from a few ratings ("fold-in"), gave odd results (horror fans got superhero films). So we changed the recipe.

Rank by your nearest favourites. For each candidate film, we measure how close it sits, in the learned embedding space, to the films you rated highest, not the average of everything you watched. We add a small nudge toward newer films and a genre anchor. The result: modern, coherent picks that actually match your taste.

Why this is the honest choice: the movie embeddings are trained on millions of ratings and are excellent. A fresh user vector built from a handful of ratings is not. So we lean on the strong part.

12 · Reflection, lessons, limits, and next

Deck: what we learned

Lessons learned

  • Collaborative signal beats content; one profile overfits
  • "Drama" tags half of all films, rank by nearest favourites
  • Honest baselines matter more than one flashy number

Known issues

  • Cold start, a brand-new viewer sits near their average
  • Tastes lean a little arthouse (it learned from Letterboxd)
  • Posters hotlink the Letterboxd CDN (fragile)
ELI5Cold start

The model barely knows a brand-new user yet, so its guesses for them are shaky until they rate more films. The fix is simply more of their history.
